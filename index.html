Trena - first, I know this is not where you wanted to be. Eight months on the market and then an expiration is exhausting, and it is easy to read it as "something must be wrong with the house." It is not. Your home actually presents beautifully, it is five years young, and it has one of the single best financing stories on the market right now.

I spent some time this morning looking at your listing the way a rate-sensitive buyer's agent would look at it. Below are the four things I think kept it from selling. No pitch. Just an honest second opinion.

1235 days is doing more damage than the price
Once a listing crosses the 60 day line, buyers start assuming something is wrong with the home. By day 235 that assumption is the main reason the showings slowed down - not the price, not the photos, and not anything about the house itself. A fresh MLS number, new photos, and a new go-to-market date is how that clock gets reset so buyers see the home the way they would have on day one.
2The price chased the market down instead of leading it
The first reduction did not come until February, roughly five months into the listing, and then three small cuts in seven weeks took you from $344K to $335K. The problem was not the final number - the problem was the signal. Small, late reductions tell the market the seller is slowly giving up, which invites lowballs instead of offers. A strategic one-time move earlier, at a cleaner round number, usually outperforms four dribbled cuts over six months.
4Your biggest advantage was buried
You are carrying an assumable VA loan at 2.7% with a payment around $1,863 a month. In a 6-7% interest rate environment, that is not a footnote - that is the headline. Right now it is sitting deep in the agent remarks instead of being the first thing every rate-shocked buyer sees when they scroll past your listing. When a relist leads with "assumable 2.7% - under $1,900 a month," the right buyer stops scrolling.
